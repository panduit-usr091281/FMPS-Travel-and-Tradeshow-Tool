Typography 

Typography Guidelines 

Make text readable and legible on different devices 
and in different lighting conditions for all languages. 


• Minimum Text Size: 16 pixels (or 11 points/1 
em) is the minimum recommended text size 
for most digital applications, as glyphs in 
non-Latin writing systems may not be 
readable below that size. 
• Text Line Height: 
-Header: 130% minimum line height for 
header text. For example, use 21-24 pixels 
line height for a 16 pixel typeface. 

-Body Copy: 160% minimum line height for 
body copy. 

-Standalone Link/Button Text: 150% 
minimum line height for body text. 

• Characters per line: 
-Desktop: 50 to 100 characters per line. 

-Mobile: 30 to 40 characters per line. 

• Multi-language consideration: Text 
translated into other languages may take 
up 30% more space than it does in English 
– plan for this in the layout. 
• Case: Use mixed case text as it is easier to 
read. 
Don’t use italicized text 

Italic text is harder to read.

ADA Contrast Guidelines 


Follow the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 
(WCAG) 2.0 for adequate contrast between text and 
background to enhance text readability. Level AA 
contrast is a base, minimum contrast requirement 
whenever text is on a background – in banners, etc. 

Minimum guidelines for contrast (WCAG Level 
AA): 

Normal text: 4.5:1 
Large text (Titles, etc.): 3:1 

Optimum contrast guidelines (WCAG Level AAA): 

Normal text: 7:1 
Large text (Titles, etc.): 4.5:1

Icons 

Descriptive Icons 

Descriptive Icons are used for visual storytelling and thematic 
representation. They convey ideas, illustrate services, or explain 
complex concepts. 

Descriptive icons are mostly non interactive, often larger and 
more detailed, providing visual support to textual content for 
example to call attention to product features. 


~~@J& 
See the Icon Library (Brand Central) for more examples or to 
get icons. 

• Size: Create icons within a 96x96 pixel frame, in SVG 
format unless otherwise specified for output (Seepage 
content guides ). 
• Padding: Provide 2-6 pixels of padding around the icon 
to prevent visual clutter. 
• Iconography Style: 
-Simplicity: Aim for straightforward designs, limit 
elements to those essential in portraying the 
meaning. 

-Limit rounded corners to convey meaning, not for 
purely stylistic purposes. 

-Use a line-based design with a consistent stroke of 
approximately 5 pixels or 5 points. Use rounded cap 
ends on lines. 

-Use complete shapes (no stylistic gaps) and avoid 

using solid fills. 

-Maintain a flat, straight-on perspective for a clean 
aesthetic (avoid 2-D or 3-D effects). 

-Color: #303030 (dark gray) for all non-interactive 
icons. 

Application icons 

Application icons serve to convey the meaning or action of 
interactive elements on the website. 

• Interactive icons typically use teal (#017698) or white 
(#FFFFFF) colors to match hyperlink labels. On hover, 
icons are filled with a tint of 30% of the icon color. 


• Icons may be filled with color for better visibility at 
smaller sizes. 
• Provide alternative for icons. 
• Maintain consistency in icon usage; Use the same icon 
for the same action, see examples in Frequently Used 
Icons. 
• Pair icons with text labels below or to the right, 
exceptions include Call-to-Action where the chevron 
icon appears to the right of the corresponding 
hyperlink. 
• Ensure vertical alignment of text and icon:

Telling a Story With Visual & Message Consistency 

Use the same visual language and 
messaging to tell a strong compelling brand 
story - Apply the same design elements 
throughout all the related assets (social media 
ads, emails, landing pages, page banners, 
product overview page, case study/white paper/ 
blog posts, print brochures, etc.) to tie them all 
together and reinforce the main campaign/ 
product/brand message. 

For example, all Apple Inc. visual 
communications have consistency regardless of 
which Apple product is represented and can be 
instantly recognized because of the strong 
brand language, even if the assets have slight 
variation for each new product launched. This is 
what we are aiming for. 

While all assets need to adhere to the overall 
Panduit brand guidelines to be recognizable as 
Panduit products, a given campaign can focus 
more on the brand design elements that best 
represent the unique product attributes. For 
example, use more black and yellow to tie in to 
the safety features of VeriSafe, or a light, clean 
and crisp look with grays and transparency for 
product “X”.

Product Hero Images 

Compelling hero images have the power to 
captivate and engage audiences. 

Hero images are large images that dominate the 
page. Use a hero image to showcase a product, to 

help tell a story and to provide context (benefits of 

the product, happy customer faces, logos, etc.), to 
help reinforce the brand, or the main message. 

Hero images are usually anchored in the most 
prominent position such as on top of the page. 
They are frequently used in banners. 

• Image Size: Depends on page template/ 
use: Hero images are used at the top of 
the page in the Featured Product/System 
Template, in the Two Column Banner and 
in the Carousel Banner on top of the 
home page 
• Show the full product, at an angle, to give 
it dimension, or show part of the product 
in a close up to highlight a feature, or add 
interest 
• Image background should be simple -
Product hero images work best either on a 
white/light gray background, or for more 
drama on a dark background, ideally with 
more dramatic lighting in the photo

Buttons 

Buttons are interactive components used to 
select options or to perform actions. Buttons are 
used in Forms and in banners or within a page 
for the Primary Call-to-Action. 


Primary Button is used for primary actions and 
primary CTAs 


Secondary Button is used on Panduit.com for

Accent Alternative Button is used only for the

Button Labels 

• Make button labels short & concise 
• Use verbs (active imperative form) & task-specific 
language, to represent the action the button will 
accomplish, for example: next’, ‘complete’, ‘start’, etc. 
• Avoid vague language that can confuse users: Use 
“Discard” and “Save” instead of “Yes” and “No” 


Short, descriptive, action oriented labels 
Avoid labels such as “Yes” and “No”; instead label 
with the expected action that will take place when 
button is clicked. 


• Use mixed case text for labels, NOT all uppercase 
letters -using uppercase letters is like shouting 
• Don’t use punctuation - no question mark or 
exclamation point -punctuation is a sign that the 
button is not standing out against other content- this is 
often because of competing design elements. 
Less is more: Keep labels to one or two words, 
don’t use punctuation or all uppercase letters

Forms: Best Practices 

Forms are known to be tedious to complete. Design forms to help 
the user complete them quickly and easily: 

Recommended background colors: white (#FFFFFF) or gray 

(#F0F0F0) 

• Use one column layout 
• Group related fields;For example, group fields related 
to personal information (First Name, Last Name) and 
fields related to contact information (Phone Number, 

Email). 

• Ask only what’s required: Shorter forms lead to 
higher conversion. 

-Try to avoid optional fields in forms, but if 
used, clearly distinguish optional fields by marketing 
them “optional” within the label 


Action Buttons 

• Use primary button styling for the primary action you 
want the user to take and secondary button styling for 
the secondary buttons/actions 
• Button Labels: See: Buttons 
Input Control Labels (see: also Input fields) 

• Use the appropriate field label for each form input 
control (input fields, drop down, check boxes, and 
radio buttons) to comply with Web Content 
Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0 


• Place labels on top of the input control & left align 
• Title case: Avoid all caps, use title case: “This Is Title 
Case” 
• No “in-line labels”, or hint text inside the text field 
• Colors/contrast: Ensure adequate contrast to comply 
with ADA Guidelines 
Feedback 

• Confirmation:Show a confirmation when the form is 
successfully completed 

• Error messages: Show message explaining corrective 
actions that user should take

Marketo Landing Pages 


Landing Pages are used in time-bound marketing 

campaigns. To be effective, they have to be: 


• Focused 
-A landing page is a standalone web 
page with a single focus: It is built 

around a specific product, target 
audience segment, promotion, and/ 
or to address a single pain point the 
target audience is facing. 

• Time Bound 
-Landing pages are NOT evergreen, 
they live for the duration of the 
campaign. 

• Have a Specific Conversion Goal 
-The design elements reflect this: 
Effective design is streamlined to 

eliminate distractions and ensure 
visitors hone in on what’s most 
important: a compelling value 
proposition and a corresponding CTA.

Landing Page Best Practices 


• Keep it simple: Deliver a single, clear, and 
compelling message 
• Focused on Target Audience: Don’t try to say 
everything to everyone - Create a Panduit.com 
page instead. 
• Quick Read: 
-Include just enough text to explain the point 
without overwhelming with too much 
information. 

-Break the text up into sections and use bullet 
points 

• Actionable Content: Address specific audience 
pain points and provide clear post-viewing actions 
for users. Social Proof: Use testimonials or call out 
statistics to increase credibility and engagement. 
• One Primary CTA: Make the most important 
intended action clear and placed front and center. 
• Create a co-branded header: Don’t place the 
Panduit logo or the Partner logo in the image or 
banner. 
• Don’t redesign the brand look and feel to try to 
get the users attention: Use good copy and one 
strong image to support your message. 
• Verify: Check mobile view and different browsers 
and check ADA compliance 
• Conduct A/B testing for continuous improvement

Gated Content Landing Page: Forms 

To capture the audience’s interest: 

• Start by briefly explaining the relevance of 
the content to their needs. 
• Incorporate quotes or statistics to 
enhance credibility and generate curiosity. 
• For gated content, emphasize why it’s 
valuable for the audience. 
• Adhere to form best practices, ensuring 
clarity and ease of completion, and 
include feedback. 
• After submission, provide a confirmation 
message along with the ability to 
download the content.

Landing Pages: Webinar/Event 

Webinar/Event Landing Pages: 

• Lead with a clear value proposition 
using the header image and title/date 
section to immediately communicate 
the webinar’s benefit, think what 

attendees will learn or gain. 

• Use scannable, benefit-driven content 
by highlighting 2–3 key takeaways using 
bullet points to improve readability 
and engagement. 
• Make the CTA stand out. Place a bold, 
action-oriented CTA such as “Reserve 
My Spot” above the fold. 


• Showcase expert presenters for 
credibility. Include headshots, titles, and 
short bios to build trust and authority, 
especially if presenters are well-known 
or industry leaders. 
• Adhere to form best practices, ensuring 
clarity and ease of completion, and 
include feedback. 
• After submission, provide a 
confirmation message along with the 

ability to download the content.

Landing Pages: Product 

Product Landing Page: 

• Above the fold must deliver a clear value 
proposition, strong headline, and CTA. 
This is where users decide whether to 
stay or bounce. 
• Use dynamic content elements, GIFs, 
videos, and interactive graphics to 
explain product features and improve 
engagement. These elements should be 
strategically placed to support the 
user journey. 
• Lead with benefits and not specs. Start 
with what the product does for the user 
i.e. solve pain points, save time, 
reduce cost.
